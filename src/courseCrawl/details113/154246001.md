##  課程簡介Course Description
The philosophies of Zhuangzi and Xunzi are pivotal contributions to both the Daoist and Confucian traditions, as well as to the broader Chinese intellectual tradition. Zhuangzi is widely regarded not only as one of the two main founders of Daoist philosophy—alongside Laozi—but also as a thinker whose influence on the Chinese intellectual landscape is profound and far-reaching. His work shaped the Chinese worldview in areas ranging from aesthetics to metaphysics, and prominent scholars such as Wang Fuzhi, Yang Rubin, and Lee Yearley have explored the deep philosophical connections between Zhuangzi and Confucianism. Yearley, in particular, has demonstrated Zhuangzi’s influence on Xunzi, showing how Xunzi’s critical engagement with Zhuangzi’s ideas led to a creative synthesis that enriched Confucian thought.
Xunzi, while highly critical of Zhuangzi’s worldview, absorbed and reinterpreted certain philosophical nuances from Zhuangzi’s thought, incorporating them into one of the most sophisticated and original frameworks within the Confucian tradition. Xunzi’s philosophy made significant contributions to ethics and political philosophy, particularly in articulating a robust moral psychology and a pragmatic approach to governance grounded in ritual (_li_) and education. His theories on human nature, moral cultivation, and institutional order have had a lasting influence on the development of Confucian ethics and the foundations of statecraft in East Asia. Together, Zhuangzi and Xunzi represent two divergent yet deeply interconnected paths of philosophical inquiry, each enriching the Chinese intellectual tradition in unique and enduring ways.
Our focus in this seminar is on uncovering the depth of these two philosophers, whose inquiries delve into the Way, politics, ethics, understanding, practical agency, and transformation—some of the central topics of the pre-Qin period. The purpose of this course is twofold: first, to delve deeply into the philosophy of both Zhuangzi and Xunzi through a rational reconstruction of their original texts within the frameworks of analytic philosophy and moral psychology; second, to demonstrate the significance and relevance of these two philosophers’ works in a contemporary context. We achieve these goals by engaging with the original texts as well as secondary literature, including the lecturers’ working manuscripts. By the end of the semester, students are expected to critically appreciate the contemporary significance of Zhuangzi’s and Xunzi’s philosophies and to conduct a research project of their own.

Physical attendance is required for students who enroll in this seminar. The teaching and discussion of this seminar will be conducted in English.

##  課程目標與學習成效Course Objectives & Learning Outcomes 
1. To understand the concepts and relevant arguments involved in the works we study.
2. To develop methods for analyzing and responding to philosophical arguments through writing and class discussions.
This course helps students familiarize themselves with key concepts and ideas in the Zhuangzi and the Xunzi by incorporating lectures, close readings of texts, question-based exercises, class discussions, and mini-seminars. These practices aim to develop students’ abilities to ask meaningful questions, think critically, and write effectively. 
By the end of the semester, students will be expected to critically appreciate the contemporary significance of the philosophy of Zhuangzi and Xunzi, and to conduct a research project of their own.
